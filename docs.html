A Time Teller without Numbers

We will start with the ordinary way of telling time from clocks like the one shown below.

At the present moment, the clock displays 4:00. We can determine this by observing the hour hand, which points to a symbol known as number 4, and the minute hand, which points to another symbol called number 12.

It appears that we need these symbols, which we call numbers, to tell time from the clock. However, as we will soon see, it is not necessary to use numbers to tell time.

We can use simple polygonal shapes to represent each hour on the clock.

To demonstrate this, let us show the time 4:00 without using numbers.

We can mark the position of the 4 on the clock with a dot, and then add 4 to itself to get 8. We draw a straight line to connect these two points on the clock.

Next, we add 4 to our previous sum of 8, which gives us 12. We connect these two points as well.

We repeat this procedure until we return to the starting point. It is worth noting that 12+4 would give us 16, but that is equivalent to 4 on our clock, right?

So, what shape do we get after connecting all of our points on the clock? A triangle! Therefore, we can define the shape of a triangle to represent 4 o'clock.

What about the minutes on the clock? We will address that shortly.

But first, let us repeat the same procedure for another hour, such as 3. Starting from 3, we add 3 to itself to get 6, then add 3 to get 9, add 3 to get 12, and finally add 3 to get back to 3. This results in a quadrilateral!

Don't you think it cool to associate 4 with a triangle and 3 with a quadrilateral?

Now, let's do the same thing for 5. Can you guess the shape? I highly recommend trying it out for yourself before reading on. It might surprise you!

And now, let's do it for 8. I know you might be feeling a bit tired by now, but it's crucial for our new time teller to represent this specific hour.

Surprisingly, we get a triangle again. Didn't we just say that a triangle defines 4 o'clock? Here's the catch: instead of only looking at the final shape, let's take a step back and consider how we actually arrived at that shape.

Notice that we joined the points in a clockwise direction to represent 4 o'clock, while we moved counterclockwise to represent 8 o'clock.

If we were to create shapes for all the hours on the clock, we would only have 8 distinct shapes. However, the direction in which we draw the shapes makes each shape unique to each hour.

Now, let's talk about the minutes. We can use the direction of the shapes being drawn to represent the minutes on our clock.

To do this, we will create a minute symbol that traverses every minute to reflect the time elapsed since the start of each hour. We will use the same direction that we identified above to traverse the minute symbol.

We now have a complete time teller (clock) that can give us the hour and minute at any given moment.
